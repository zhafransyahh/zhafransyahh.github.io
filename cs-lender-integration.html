01 / The Stakes

A critical funding gap, and no safety net

When I was assigned to this project, our funding situation was critical. Expanding our institutional funding base was a strategic priority, diversifying our lending capacity and creating room for controlled, sustainable growth. The pressure to deliver was immediate.

To make things harder: the PM leading this project transitioned off mid-way, with no formal handover. I inherited fragmented documentation, fragmented institutional knowledge, and a project already in motion, and was expected to drive it to completion.

It was my first time leading an integration of this scale. I had to learn everything on the fly.

03 / Scope of Ownership

Not a single-track PM role, an orchestrator

This wasn't a single-track PM role. I was the orchestrator across the entire integration lifecycle, coordinating across engineering, QA, business, and an external institutional partner simultaneously.

04 / Money Flow Architecture

Designing how large-scale institutional funds move

Mapping the money flow was one of the most consequential design decisions in the project. Errors in disbursement routing or repayment settlement at this scale would cascade instantly. The architecture had to be airtight before a single line of code was written.

The lender's pivot to API-based disbursement - mandated mid-project, meant redesigning the outbound money flow from scratch while keeping repayment settlement on SFTP. We had no bargaining power over the protocol choice; institutional lenders set the terms. The result is a hybrid architecture where each transaction direction uses the channel the lender requires.

05 / The Pivot

Partial architectural reset: no bargaining power

Midway through the project, after the SFTP-based flow had already been scoped and largely designed, the lender requested a shift to API-based disbursement. Repayment settlement would remain SFTP - but the disbursement leg, where funds flow from the lender to the platform, had to be rebuilt as an API integration. It pushed the timeline back by one to two months and required re-aligning the entire team's understanding of the outbound money flow architecture.

With no assumptions to fall back on, I had to reason through every decision from first principles, and document everything as I went, building the knowledge base that hadn't existed when I inherited the project.

This is where having no prior experience in this type of integration actually sharpened my instincts. No prior mental models meant no prior assumptions to protect.

06 / Post-Launch

Going live was not the finish line

Going live wasn't the finish line. A hybrid API/SFTP integration requires continuous operational attention across both channels: API callback success rates on the disbursement side, SFTP file integrity and delivery confirmation on the settlement side, reconciliation across both, and exception handling throughout.

I maintained ownership of this through post-launch, identifying and resolving operational issues as they surfaced, ensuring disbursements continued running without disruption. Uptime has remained above OJK-licensed platform SLA thresholds since go-live, with no critical production failures.

07 / Live Data: Channel Impact

From 0% to ~40% of total monthly disbursement

The chart below shows the institutional lender channel's share of total platform monthly disbursement by amount since go-live, calculated from internal data as a percentage ratio. Absolute figures are withheld. The channel ramped from a soft pilot in August to representing close to 40% of total platform volume within six months, becoming one of the platform's largest single funding sources alongside other active institutional channels.

The channel also skews toward higher average ticket sizes; its share of disbursement by amount consistently runs 4–6 percentage points above its share by loan count, which itself stabilized at ~33% of total monthly loans by Q1 2026. The channel's average ticket size runs approximately 17% above platform average, consistent with a slightly higher-value borrower segment being served through the institutional channel.

What I Took Away

The hardest part of this project wasn't the technical complexity - it was operating with confidence in a high-stakes environment where I had no safety net, no prior experience, and no complete map of the territory. What carried me through was treating every unknown as a structured problem: identify what I don't know, find who does, document what I learn, and keep the integration moving forward. That approach - more than any specific skill - is what I'd take into any complex cross-functional project.
